## LinkedIn

### Variant A — Narrative hook

Most "what is an AI receptionist" articles online are written by vendors and end with a sales pitch.

None of them lead with the section that actually matters: what an AI receptionist still can't do.

Here's the honest list:

It can't read tone the way a senior front-desk hire can. It shouldn't handle clinical or legal advice without escalating to a human. It can't make judgment calls outside the rules you give it. And it doesn't replace the rapport your repeat customers feel when they hear a familiar voice.

What it does do, reliably, today: answer 24/7 with sub-3-second pickup, book directly into your calendar, qualify leads, warm-transfer urgent calls with a spoken summary, and log every conversation with a transcript.

The category isn't magic and it isn't experimental. It's a powerful, narrow tool. Deploy it on the parts of your call queue that are bleeding (after-hours, lunch-hour, busy-line drops) and let your humans own the calls that need real judgment.

A good rule of thumb: if your front desk misses more than 10% of calls or works past hours to keep up, AI pays for itself in a month. If your missed-call rate is under 5% and your customers know your receptionist by name, the upgrade isn't urgent.

### Variant B — Data/insight

A human receptionist runs $3,000-$4,500/month fully loaded. A live answering service charges $1.50-$3.00 per minute and takes messages — it doesn't book.

An AI receptionist sits in a different category. Flat monthly fee in the $200-$800 range, books appointments on the call, qualifies leads, warm-transfers with context, and works 24/7.

For most small businesses that math is 70-90% cheaper than a human, roughly the same as a live service, while doing more on every call.

The tradeoff: AI can't read tone the way a senior front-desk hire can, and it shouldn't handle clinical or legal advice without escalating. The right pattern is hybrid — AI covers the volume floor and after-hours, the human owns the relationship hours and the calls that need judgment.

If your inbound queue is bleeding (after-hours misses, lunch-hour drops, busy-line hangups), the recovered bookings in the first 30 days typically cover the cost of the service several times over.
