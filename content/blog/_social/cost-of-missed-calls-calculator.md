### Variant A — Narrative hook

A 4-operatory dental clinic missing 6 calls a day with a 55% phone-to-patient conversion rate is losing $61,710 a month.

Most owners run that math once and don't sleep that night.

Pull up your call records from last week. Count the voicemails, the hang-ups, the "no answer" lines. Multiply by your average new customer value, your typical phone conversion rate, and your working days per month.

The number you get is what your phone cost you last month.

The $75-per-missed-call figure most operators repeat — the one from the 2010 Forbes piece — is wrong for almost every business. It's wrong because it's a national average across industries that have nothing in common. It's wrong because it only counts the immediate transaction. It misses four hidden costs:

1. Lifetime value loss. A new dental patient is $800 in year one but $4,000-$6,000 over five years. The missed call didn't cost you the cleaning. It cost you the cleanings, the crown three years from now, the family that would have followed.

2. Referral cascade. Roughly 1 in 3 new customers in service businesses comes from referral. The customer you lost would have generated 0.3-0.6 referrals over their lifetime. That's another 30-60% on top of the original loss.

3. Negative review risk. Customers who hit voicemail and don't get a callback within 30 minutes are 4x more likely to leave a one-star review. The review impact often dwarfs the missed call itself.

4. Marketing waste. If you spend $2,000/month on Google Ads to make the phone ring and you miss 20% of calls, $400 of that is funding voicemail.

The cheapest call you'll ever take is the one that books itself.

- Most small businesses miss 15-30% of inbound calls. Above 25% is a structural problem, not a staffing one.
- Hiring an answering service typically disappoints within 90 days because services take messages — they don't book
